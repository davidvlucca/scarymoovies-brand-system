# Introduction

## What Is ScaryMoovies

ScaryMoovies is a horror-first movie platform built for people who take the genre seriously. At its core, it combines the cataloguing depth of IMDB with the social intelligence of Letterboxd — but strips out everything that isn't horror. No action blockbusters bleeding into the recommended feed. No comedy-horrors diluting the browse experience. No algorithm trained on a general audience deciding what a horror fan should watch next.

The platform is organized around four capabilities that work together: **Discovery**, which surfaces the right film at the right moment through editorial curation and mood-based matching; **Community**, which lets users build watchlists, write reviews, track activity, and engage with other obsessives; **Tier List**, a structured rating tool that maps personal taste across seven tiers from S down to F; and **Pick Your Fear**, an eight-question quiz that reads your current mood, environment, and tolerance for specific horror elements and returns a single confident recommendation.

## Why It Exists

The horror genre has no home that takes it seriously from the inside.

Netflix carries horror, but it is a genre tab buried inside a mass-market platform that treats a slasher the same way it treats a cooking competition. IMDB is a comprehensive database, but it is encyclopaedic rather than experiential — it will tell you cast and crew, but it has no opinion, no curation, no sense of what it means to love the genre. Letterboxd has a passionate horror community, but it is a general film journal first and was never designed around the particular way horror fans navigate taste: by sub-genre, by mood, by fear type, by the specific kind of dread a film delivers.

The gap is real: there is no dedicated horror platform with editorial curation, community tools, and mood-aware discovery built into a single coherent experience. ScaryMoovies exists to close that gap.

## Who It Is For

ScaryMoovies is built for three distinct but overlapping users.

The **horror obsessive** knows the difference between giallo and slasher, has opinions about the post-2010 elevated horror wave, and wants a platform that speaks their language — one that surfaces deep cuts and rare international titles alongside mainstream releases, and lets them log, tier, and debate everything they watch.

The **casual horror watcher** comes to the genre seasonally or situationally — Halloween, a specific mood, a friend's recommendation — and needs help navigating a genre that can feel overwhelming or inconsistent in quality. Pick Your Fear exists primarily for them: a guided path from "I want something scary tonight" to a single reliable recommendation.

The **community reviewer** treats watching as a social act. They write reviews, follow other users, post to the activity feed, and build their taste profile publicly over time. They want their watchlist and tier rankings to be visible, their reviews to find readers, and their opinions to matter inside a community that shares the same obsession.


## Platform Pillars

**Discovery** is the editorial layer — the hero section, featured titles, coming-soon releases, trending content, and collections organized by sub-genre. Curation is intentional and horror-literate, not algorithmic noise.

**Community** is the social layer — user profiles, watchlists, star-and-text reviews, an activity feed, and an achievement system that rewards engagement milestones automatically. It is built on the assumption that horror watching is a shared experience, not a solitary one.

**Pick Your Fear** is the recommendation engine expressed as a conversational quiz. Eight questions read the viewer's current state — mood, environment, tolerance for specific fear categories — and return one film, not fifty. Confidence over volume.

The **Tier List** sits across all three pillars. It is personal (your taste map), social (visible to followers), and discovery-adjacent (tier rankings surface patterns across the community).

## What This Brand System Covers

This document is the authoritative reference for how ScaryMoovies looks, speaks, and behaves across every surface. It is organized into ten sections:

1. **Introduction** — this document: vision, audience, pillars, and system overview
2. **Brand Foundation** — mission, values, personality, and the tone-of-voice principles that govern all copy
3. **Visual Language** — the aesthetic philosophy: dark-first, cinematic, horror-authentic
4. **Color System** — the full palette, semantic tokens, dark-mode defaults, and usage rules
5. **Typography** — typeface selection, scale, hierarchy, and rendering guidelines
6. **Iconography** — the icon set, usage conventions, and production specs
7. **Components** — the UI component library with usage guidelines and variant documentation
8. **Motion** — animation principles, timing, and transition patterns
9. **Voice and Copy** — writing guidelines for UI copy, review prompts, achievement names, and editorial content
10. **Platform Features** — feature-specific design and copy guidance for Tier List, Pick Your Fear, Achievements, and the Activity Feed

Designers, engineers, and content contributors should use this system as the single source of truth. Where a decision is not covered, the guiding question is: would a serious horror fan find this authentic?

## A Note on the Aesthetic

ScaryMoovies is not a generic dark UI with red accents. It is not trying to look spooky. It does not lean on clichés — no dripping blood fonts, no jump-scare gimmicks, no camp.

The aesthetic is cinematic and deliberately understated: deep blacks, carefully considered contrast, typography that reads like a film title card, interactions that have weight and purpose. The darkness is not decoration — it is the correct environment for the content. Horror films are meant to be watched in the dark. The platform reflects that.

The tone is that of a knowledgeable, serious fan who has no patience for the genre being dismissed and no interest in performing edginess. It is confident, specific, and literate about horror. Every copy decision, color choice, and interaction pattern should be traceable back to that position.
