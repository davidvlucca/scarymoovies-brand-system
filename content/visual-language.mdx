# Visual Language

ScaryMoovies looks the way it looks for a reason. This page explains the "why" behind every visual decision — the aesthetic philosophy that separates this platform from generic dark UI and ties it to the specific visual tradition of horror cinema.

---

## Why Dark-First

Horror lives in the dark. Not because darkness is trendy — it's been the setting of the genre since the first shadow crept across a silent film screen. ScaryMoovies is dark because the films it documents are dark. The background is never "night mode" applied to a light design. It is the starting condition. Light is the exception. Everything that is illuminated — a film title, a rating, a user's name — earns its visibility against the dark.

**Root background: `#0c0a0e`**. Not pure black. A black with a bruised purple undertone. Warm enough to feel organic. Cool enough to feel dangerous.

<PullQuote>
  The design is not dark. The design is horror. The distinction matters every time you reach for a token.
</PullQuote>

---

## Why Not Generic Dark SaaS

This is the most important distinction in the entire system.

Any product can apply a dark color palette and call it "night mode." Generic dark SaaS looks like:

- Pure `#000000` or `#1a1a1a` black with no character
- Bright blue or teal accent colors
- Clean geometric sans-serif everything
- Gradients as decoration
- Glow effects for "premium" feel

ScaryMoovies avoids every one of these. The accent is **desaturated purple** — not the victorious electric purple of productivity tools, but the deep bruise-purple of *Suspiria*, of old VHS boxes, of theatrical velvet. The display font is **Creepster** — explicitly horror-coded. The grain is **atmospheric**, not decorative.

## The Three Visual Moods

Every screen in ScaryMoovies exists within one of three visual moods. Understanding them is the foundation for making design decisions at the component level.

### Cinematic

Formal. Still. Referencing the title card tradition of horror cinema — the deliberate composition, the typography that announces rather than decorates. Used for editorial moments: the Pick Your Fear quiz, landing screens, section openers.

**Visual cues:** Centered composition · Playfair Display at large scale · letterboxed imagery · very little motion

### Atmospheric

Immersive. Textured. Edges bleed into dark. Used for browse contexts — the Explore grid, collection views, the homepage scroll. The interface recedes; the posters breathe.

**Visual cues:** Poster art dominant · minimal chrome · gradient fades to `--bg-primary` · subtle grain overlay

### Dread

Tense. Immediate. High contrast. Used sparingly — error states, confirmation dialogs, destructive actions. The red (`--accent-danger: #751111`) is not an error color in the conventional UX sense. It is the color of something that matters.

**Visual cues:** `--accent-danger` borders or backgrounds · direct language · no softening · reduced spacing

**What these images share:**

- **Grain** — analog, not digital. The noise is part of the image, not a bug.

- **Stillness** — nothing is moving. The tension is in the composition itself.

## Texture and Grain

Horror has always been a textured medium. Grain in horror photography and film is not a flaw — it is the material evidence of darkness captured. ScaryMoovies uses grain deliberately in atmospheric imagery as a signal: *this is that tradition.*

The `AtmosphericImage` component provides a CSS noise overlay via the `grain` prop. Use it on hero images, section backgrounds, and mood-defining contexts. Do not apply it to UI components, diagrams, or spec illustrations.

| Context | Grain |
|---|---|
| Hero / banner images | Yes — always |
| Moodboard | Yes |
| Film posters | No — preserve original art |
| UI components | Never |
| Screen annotations | Never |

## Light and Shadow

**Elevation is expressed through darkness, not light.** In most design systems, elevated surfaces get lighter. In ScaryMoovies, they get slightly more opaque against the primary background:

| Layer | Token | Hex |
|---|---|---|
| Background | `--bg-primary` | `#0c0a0e` |
| Surfaces (cards, panels) | `--bg-surface` | `#0b090d` |
| Elevated (modals, dropdowns) | `--bg-elevated` | `#3d3b3e` |

The jump from surface to elevated is intentional — `--bg-elevated` is significantly lighter to create readable layering for floating UI elements.

**Shadows are dark and deep.** Modal overlays use `rgba(12,10,14,0.85)` with blur. Dropdown shadows use `0 12px 32px rgba(0,0,0,0.5)`. Never use box shadows in white or light tones.

## What ScaryMoovies Is Not

To protect the visual language, here is what it explicitly rejects:

**Not a dark-mode toggle.** There is no light version. Do not design for one.

**Not edgy-for-edgy's-sake.** The aesthetic is grounded in a specific cinematic tradition. A skull icon, a splatter texture, or a horror stock photo does not automatically fit — it must feel like it belongs to the same visual vocabulary.

**Not generic streaming.** The palette is intentionally distinct from Netflix dark-gray, HBO dark-navy, and Apple TV near-black. The purple undertone is the differentiator. Protect it.

**Not retro-fetish.** VHS grain and analog textures are used as a nod to the era when horror found its identity. Not as irony. Not as trend. Use them where they reinforce the tradition, not as decoration.
