# Voice & Tone

ScaryMoovies is for people who take horror seriously. The writing reflects that — atmospheric, specific, and never condescending about the genre or the audience. We write like a fellow obsessive, not a platform trying to sell you something.

---

## Brand Voice

### 1. Dark and Atmospheric

We set a mood before we sell a film. Every piece of copy — even microcopy — should feel like it belongs to the world of horror. Not wallowing in darkness for shock's sake, but always aware that the genre lives in atmosphere.

> *"It's past midnight. You've made your choices. Now the film begins."*

### 2. Reverent Toward the Genre

Horror is an art form. We talk about it that way. We don't wink at the audience or treat the genre as inherently ridiculous. We can acknowledge that a slasher is a slasher — we just respect what a great one does.

> *"Argento understood that color could be as terrifying as shadow. Suspiria doesn't scare you — it infects you."*

### 3. Insider and Direct

We speak like someone who has seen too many horror films and is glad for it. References land without over-explaining. We trust the reader. We don't soften our language to make the genre palatable to people who aren't the audience.

> *"If you haven't seen Hereditary's third act in the dark, alone, you haven't really seen it."*

### 4. Specific and Visceral

Vague praise is worthless. We describe what a film actually does — what kind of dread it builds, where the tension lives, whether the horror is psychological or physical or both. The writing earns the recommendation.

> *"The haunting here is slow. Forty minutes of nothing wrong, then a door that shouldn't be open."*

### 5. Slightly Poetic About Mood

Not overwrought. Not purple prose. But we're allowed one well-placed image that makes the reader feel something before they've even hit play.

> *"The kind of film you remember at 3am for no reason. The kind where you check the locks."*

## Writing Principles

### 1. Be specific, not superlative

Bad: *"One of the scariest films ever made."*
Good: *"The final twenty minutes don't let you breathe. You will not sleep after the barn scene."*

### 2. Name the type of horror

Audiences need to know what they're signing up for. Psychological dread is different from body horror is different from atmospheric slow-burn. Name it.

Bad: *"A terrifying experience that will leave you shaken."*
Good: *"Psychological horror built on grief and guilt — not a jump scare in sight."*

### 3. Don't explain the joke (or the fear)

If something is eerie, let it be eerie. Don't follow an ominous line with a clarifying sentence that defangs it.

Bad: *"Your tier list is empty. That's because you haven't added any films yet — get started!"*
Good: *"Your tier list is empty. The horror hasn't found you yet."*

### 4. Avoid the wink

Campy self-awareness belongs in a different brand. We never make fun of the genre or signal that we're above it. No ironic quotation marks around horror tropes. No "things are about to get scary" energy.

Bad: *"Warning: this one's a real BOO-lever!"*
Good: *"This one doesn't let go."*

### 5. Write for the dark

Imagine the reader is watching alone, late at night. That's the real audience. Write for that moment, not for a product page.

Bad: *"Browse our curated horror content for all preferences."*
Good: *"Whatever you're afraid of, it's in here somewhere."*

### 6. Earn every adjective

Don't stack modifiers. One precise word beats three vague ones.

Bad: *"A deeply unsettling, atmospheric, and genuinely terrifying slow-burn horror experience."*
Good: *"A slow-burn that earns every minute of its dread."*

## Tone by Context

### Homepage Hero
Atmospheric and cinematic. This is the establishing shot. Set the mood before asking for anything.

> *"Horror doesn't entertain you. It finds something true about fear and makes you sit with it. Welcome."*

### Movie Descriptions
Specific, visceral, honest. Tell the reader what kind of experience they're choosing. Don't oversell — if a film is flawed but important, say so. Trust the reader to decide.

> *"Messy in the third act, but the first hour is some of the finest slow-burn dread in contemporary horror. The sound design alone warrants a watch."*

### Pick Your Fear Questions
Ominous, direct, conspiratorial. The questions should feel like an interrogation by someone who already knows too much about you.

- "What time is it?" — not "When are you watching?"
- "Are you alone?" — not "Is anyone watching with you?"
- "How much can you handle tonight?" — not "What intensity level do you prefer?"
- "What are you afraid of?" — not "What genre interests you?"

### Achievement Notifications
Proud and dark-celebratory. Acknowledge the accomplishment without breaking voice.

> *"You've survived 100 films. Most people stop at ten."*
> *"Completed: The Slow Burn shelf. You have patience. Horror rewards patience."*

### Empty States
Eerie and inviting. The absence of content should still feel like it belongs to the brand.

> *"Your watchlist is empty. Something's coming. It always is."*
> *"No films rated yet. The darkness hasn't taken shape."*

### Error Messages
Matter-of-fact with a trace of the brand's atmosphere. Don't be cute. Don't be corporate.

> *"Something went wrong in the dark. Refresh and try again."*
> *"We lost the signal. It happens. Come back."*

## Vocabulary to Use

Words and phrases that are authentic to the genre and the brand:

- **Dread** — sustained, low-level fear; the mood before the scare
- **Atmospheric** — when the setting does as much work as the plot
- **Visceral** — physical, immediate, bodily
- **Haunting** — something that stays with you after the film ends
- **Relentless** — no relief, no release
- **Subtext** — horror's capacity to be about something real underneath
- **Tension** — the architecture of a well-built scare sequence
- **Slow-burn** — a film that earns its horror gradually
- **Unsettling** — discomfort that doesn't resolve into a clean scare
- **Psychological** — the horror lives in the mind, not the monster
- **Body horror** — when the fear is physical, transformative, biological
- **Dread** — use this over "scary" when the fear is ambient, not acute

## Vocabulary to Avoid

### Generic streaming/platform language
- "Explore our vast catalog"
- "Curated content for every viewer"
- "Your personalized horror experience"
- "Discover something new"
- "Premium selection"
- "All the horror you love, in one place"

### Campy horror clichés
- "Things are getting SPOOKtacular"
- "Dying to see more?"
- "Drop-dead good picks"
- "Frighteningly good"
- "To die for"
- "Hair-raising" (unless used without irony in a description)

### Pretentious film-school speak
- "Deconstructing the male gaze through the horror lens"
- "A meta-textual examination of genre tropes"
- Avoid academic hedging and over-qualification in general

## Example Rewrites

**1. Onboarding welcome**
Before: *"Welcome to ScaryMoovies! Explore thousands of horror films curated just for you."*
After: *"You're here. That means you already know what you're looking for. Let's find it."*

**2. Film description (The Shining)**
Before: *"A classic psychological horror film starring Jack Nicholson that will keep you on the edge of your seat."*
After: *"Kubrick's adaptation doesn't haunt the way King's novel does — it's colder, more architectural. The Overlook isn't just dangerous. It's patient."*

**3. Empty watchlist**
Before: *"Your watchlist is empty! Start adding films to keep track of what you want to watch."*
After: *"Nothing here yet. Whatever you're afraid of, it hasn't found a name."*

**4. Achievement unlock**
Before: *"Congratulations! You've watched 50 horror films on ScaryMoovies!"*
After: *"50 films. You've seen things. Some of them stay."*

**5. Error message**
Before: *"Oops! Something went wrong. Please try again later."*
After: *"Something went wrong in the dark. Try again."*
